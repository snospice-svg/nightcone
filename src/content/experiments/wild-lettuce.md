Lactuca virosa produces a milky sap that dries to a dark resin nineteenth-century physicians called "lettuce opium." It isn't an opiate and doesn't bind opioid receptors; the active compounds — lactucin and lactucopicrin, sesquiterpene lactones — have a poorly understood, possibly GABAergic, mildly sedative action.

Protocol: roughly 300 mg of resin extract in hot water, 45 minutes before bed, fourteen nights. The taste is reliably, intensely bitter. Sleep onset *felt* a little faster by night three, but that could be placebo. Sedation was mild at best.

Recall didn't change in the first week; vividness was unremarkable. Around night nine there were slightly longer, more continuous dream sequences — but inconsistently, and some nights nothing. No lucidity at any point. The honest summary: the "lettuce opium" reputation massively oversells this plant. If it touches dreaming, the effect sits below what a 14-night protocol can resolve without instrumentation.
