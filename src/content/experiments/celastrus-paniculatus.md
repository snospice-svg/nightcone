A climbing shrub native to India, used in Ayurveda for centuries as a cognitive tonic — the seed oil is called Jyotishmati, roughly "luminous." The seeds carry sesquiterpenes and alkaloids; animal studies suggest the oil may raise acetylcholine availability, which is the thread that makes it interesting for dreaming. That link has never been tested in humans for dream effects, so the rationale is extrapolation.

Protocol: cold-pressed seed oil, ten drops in warm water, thirty minutes before sleep, fourteen consecutive nights. The first few nights were quiet. By the middle of the run, recall sat slightly above baseline and dreams felt denser — more detail packed into shorter sequences.

Night 9 was the outlier: unusually high visual detail — textures, lighting, spatial depth — not lucid, but rendered at a noticeably higher resolution than baseline. The effect then plateaued. The cholinergic hypothesis is plausible but unconfirmed, and the Ayurvedic tradition emphasises months of use, so fourteen nights may undersell it.
