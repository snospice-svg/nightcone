The African dream bean: a large seed from a tropical climbing vine, used by traditional healers across southern Africa to induce vivid dreams, often framed as communication with the ancestors. The inner meat is the active part; it contains alkaloids, saponins, and essential oils, and there is no clinical literature on its effect on sleep or dreams. Everything available is ethnobotanical and anecdotal.

Protocol: dried, ground inner seed meat, roughly one gram with water, thirty minutes before sleep, fourteen nights. The first four nights were flat. From night five, recall rose on maybe three nights in five — dreams a little longer, vividness marginal, hard to separate from expectation.

Night ten stood out: a dream with unusual structural coherence — continuous narrative, consistent setting, no scene jumps — closer to the lucid threshold than usual, though not lucid. It happened once and never repeated. By the end: modest, inconsistent recall, no reliable lucidity, sleep unaffected. The traditional preparations may combine this with techniques a chemical-only test deliberately strips out.
